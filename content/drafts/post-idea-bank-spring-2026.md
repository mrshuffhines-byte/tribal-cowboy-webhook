# Post Idea Bank — Spring 2026

*Generated from weekly competitor research, March 30, 2026. Pull from these anytime. Organized by content pillar.*

---

## The Animals (Size, Personality, Herd Life)

**1. "What 1,800 pounds looks like next to a 4-year-old"**
Reel. Side-by-side or video of a kid meeting Millie or Abby for the first time. The size difference is the content. Keep the caption grounded — the kid's name (with permission), the horse's name, what actually happened. No "magical moment" language. The photo does that work.
*Research source: Clydesdale Outpost's most engaging format.*

**2. "Two generations, one paddock"**
Carousel or Reel. Belle and Millie (mother-daughter). Or Jill and Abby. Show them together, note the lineage. "Same Canadian breeder, same barn, very different opinions about breakfast." Horse-knowledgeable audiences love lineage content. Breed enthusiasts share it.

**3. "Monster has identified the person most likely to give him a snack"**
Reel. Follow Monster as he walks the fence line scanning for the softest touch. The mini gelding working a crowd is inherently funny. Let him be the comedian of the herd.

**4. "The loading order argument (year three)"**
Reel. Abby and Jolene's ongoing disagreement about who gets on the trailer first. The size contrast between them. Neither one concedes. Personal account energy — unscripted, a little funny.

**5. "Horse personality chart"**
Carousel (5-7 slides). Each horse gets a slide with their name, a photo, and one sentence about their personality. "Millie: will stand still for anything except a bath." "Monster: has never met a stranger and never will." People save these and come back to them.

## Behind-the-Scenes Work

**6. "5 AM haul day"**
Reel. Loading the trailer, the drive, the setup. The contrast between the labor and the guest experience is the emotional hook. Text overlay on the last frame: "They just see the horses." Nobody in the pony party industry shows this.
*Research source: Content gap across ALL competitor accounts.*

**7. "What goes in the trailer"**
Carousel or Reel. Open the trailer doors, show everything that gets packed. Grooming supplies, branded overlays, photo equipment, safety gear, treats, water. The logistics of showing up professional. Corporate event planners notice this level of detail.

**8. "Grooming ASMR"**
Reel (sound-on). Close-up grooming footage. Brush strokes, hoof picking, mane braiding. All real audio. The sounds are the hook. ASMR-adjacent animal content is trending hard. Also strong for equine care brand partnerships.

**9. "Before and after: empty field to full event"**
Reel (time-lapse or side-by-side). Empty venue in the morning, full setup with horses by event time. Shows professionalism and effort without saying a word about it.

---

## Facial Recognition / Photo Tech

**10. "You didn't even know we took this one"**
Reel. Screen record the QR scan, show photos loading with branded overlay. Cut to the guest's face reacting. This is the single biggest differentiator and nobody else is talking about it. Revisit this format monthly with different event footage.
*Research source: Zero competitors offer this tech.*

**11. "What the event planner sees vs. what the guest sees"**
Carousel. Split screen or alternating slides. Planner: branded photos with their logo on every image, delivered automatically. Guest: their photos on their phone before they reach the car. Both sides win. Corporate-forward.

**12. "This is what every photo looks like when it leaves a TC event"**
Reel or single image. Show the finished product — a real event photo with the branded overlay. Event name, logo, date. Clean, professional, shareable. Explain in 2 sentences what this means for a company booking an event.

## Indigenous Identity

**13. "What people ask me when I say I'm Nisenan Maidu and I run a horse business"**
Talking-head Reel. Address real questions Stacie gets. Not educational in a lecture way — honest, warm, a little funny if the moment allows. Nobody in the PNW horse experience space is doing this kind of content. The lane is wide open.
*Research source: Indigenous-owned outdoor business category is nearly empty on U.S. Instagram.*

**14. "This land, this business"**
Single photo or Reel. What it means to run this business on this specific land. Shot of the property at golden hour. Caption is Stacie's own words — not scripted by anyone else.

---

## North Idaho Life

**15. "Late April in Athol"**
Single photo. Minimal caption. Two or three words. The landscape does the work. Strong seasonal visual for outdoor/western lifestyle brands. Save the pretty shots for this pillar — don't waste them on posts where a caption carries the weight.

**16. "The horses know the season before the forecast does"**
Reel or photo. Spring behavior changes — coats shedding, grazing patterns shifting, the first morning they're all the way out in the field instead of huddled by the barn. Real observation, not poetic.

---

## Education + Credibility

**17. "Here's everything that shows up when you book a pony party"**
Reel (walkthrough). Calm, professional, informational. What TC handles, what the host doesn't have to worry about. This is the corporate-quality explainer that event planners share with their boss.

**18. "Three things about bringing a 1,800-lb Clydesdale to a corporate parking lot"**
Talking head or text-over-video. Practical, funny if the moment warrants it. What goes right, what goes sideways, what people don't think about. Direct energy.

**19. "Why we don't use rented horses"**
Reel or carousel. The answer: because a horse that knows you and trusts you behaves differently around a crowd of strangers. A rented horse is unpredictable. Our horses have done this hundreds of times. Safety, consistency, temperament. This is what separates TC from every mobile pony party company.
*Research source: NW Pony Parties, Pony Town Parties, and Giddyup Farms all use their own horses — but none of them are making content about WHY that matters.*
