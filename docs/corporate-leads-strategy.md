## Corporate Buyer Overview

Corporate buyers for equine experiences are not who you might expect. They are not ranchers or horse people. They are:

- HR Directors and People Operations managers looking for team event alternatives that feel fresh
- Event coordinators at companies with 20–500 employees
- Executive assistants who handle logistics for leadership retreats
- Marketing managers planning client entertainment events
- Company owners in North Idaho and Eastern Washington who want something local and distinctive

The decision cycle is longer than consumer bookings — typically 4–12 weeks from first contact to deposit. The decision maker and the booking contact are often different people. Budget authority usually sits with HR, Operations, or the executive team.

**What they are buying:** They are not buying a pony ride. They are buying a story they can tell their team — something that says "this company does things differently." The Clydesdale wagon and the horses are props for that story.

## What Corporate Buyers Need to See

Corporate buyers are risk-averse. They are spending company money on an event that reflects on them personally. They need:

**1. Proof you have done this before.**
Photos, a recap, a testimonial from a real company. Even one documented corporate event is enough to break the "first-time buyer" hesitation.

**2. Clear logistics answer.**
How many people can TC accommodate? What is the minimum group size? What is included? How far will TC travel? Is setup handled? What is the rain plan?

**3. A professional point of contact.**
Corporate buyers expect a real email response within 24 hours, ideally a proposal document or information sheet, and a clear next step.

**4. Easy to explain to their boss.**
If an EA is booking this, they need to be able to summarize it in 2 sentences to the executive: "It's a 2-hour equine experience — wagon rides, horse interaction, and custom photos — at our team retreat." Make that pitch easy.

**5. Pricing they can put in a budget request.**
Do not make corporate buyers ask twice for pricing. Give them a starting rate and package options. "Starting at $XXX for groups up to 25" is enough to get the conversation moving.

**6. Something they can show people.**
Video or photos that show the experience. TC's social media is the portfolio — make sure corporate-appropriate content exists and is findable.

## Message Framework for Corporate Audiences

Consumer messaging is emotional. Corporate messaging is logical with an emotional close.

### Structure
1. **Problem** — "Team events in North Idaho options are mostly bars and golf courses."
2. **Differentiator** — "TC is a working Indigenous-owned equine operation. The experience is real, not a themed rental."
3. **Proof** — One sentence about a real event or capability.
4. **Outcome** — "Your team leaves with a story, professional photos, and something to actually talk about."
5. **Next step** — Simple, no pressure. "Happy to send more info or jump on a 15-minute call."

### Tone
Warm professionalism. Not stiff. Not formal. But more direct and outcome-focused than consumer content. Corporate buyers respond to specificity — group sizes, logistics, what happens when, what is handled for them.

### What Corporate Copy Never Sounds Like
- "We craft unforgettable team-building memories"
- "An experience unlike anything else"
- "Perfect for your next corporate event"
- Any sentence that could apply to a bowling alley

## Corporate Experience Positioning

TC's corporate offering should be positioned as a *premium team experience*, not a party service.

### The Frame
"Tribal Cowboy brings a working Clydesdale and pony herd to your event or retreat. Guests experience authentic North Idaho — wagon rides, horse interaction, and professional photos delivered to their phones the same day via facial recognition. It is not a carnival ride. It is a real experience with real animals, run by a real operator who has been doing this in this region."

### What Makes TC Distinctly Corporate-Ready
- **instant photo delivery** — Guests get their photos instantly on their phones. No emailing, no hunting for a photographer, no ordering prints. This feature sells itself to corporate buyers who want to give guests a tangible takeaway.
- **Custom branded overlays** — Event name, company logo, date on every photo. This is a company-branded deliverable, not just photos.
- **Professional setup and teardown** — TC handles logistics. The corporate client does not need to manage the animals.
- **Insurance and liability** — Have this documented and ready to share. Corporate buyers will ask.
- **Indigenous ownership** — Authentic, meaningful differentiator for companies with DEI values or ESG commitments. Do not lead with this in cold outreach — but it adds depth when the relationship is established.

## Outreach Strategy

### LinkedIn (Primary Corporate Outreach Channel)
LinkedIn is where corporate buyers live. The strategy here is connection before pitch.

**Profile requirement:** Tribal Cowboy should have a LinkedIn Company Page, and Stacie should have a personal LinkedIn profile listing her as Owner.

**Content on LinkedIn:**
- Post 1–2 times per week: event recaps, behind-the-scenes, professional photos
- Use LinkedIn Article format for longer pieces about equine team experiences
- Engage genuinely with North Idaho and Spokane business community posts

**Connection outreach:**
- Connect with HR directors, event coordinators, and executive assistants at target companies
- Personalized connection note — reference their company or role specifically
- Wait until accepted before any pitch — do not pitch in the connection request
- 1 week after connecting, send a warm intro message (see template below)

### LinkedIn Content — What's Different From Instagram

LinkedIn corporate buyers see different content than TC's Instagram audience. The same post that works beautifully on Instagram ("Duke showed up to a corporate event and had three grown adults reconsidering their life choices") works on LinkedIn too — but LinkedIn needs more.

**What to post on LinkedIn that you would not post on Instagram:**
- Event recap with a professional framing: group size, company type (no names without permission), what they did, what the instant photo delivery looked like
- Short first-person piece from Stacie: "What I've learned running equine events for corporate groups" — positions her as an expert, not a vendor
- Behind-the-logistics content: what it takes to show up with a Clydesdale at a corporate venue (setup, insurance, timing) — this shows competence to corporate buyers
- Indigenous-owned business visibility posts: these get significantly higher reach on LinkedIn and attract both corporate buyers with DEI commitments and media/press attention
- Client appreciation photo posts: tag the company (with permission) after an event — the company's network sees TC

**What NOT to post on LinkedIn:**
- Pony party content (audience skews corporate — keep it B2B)
- Pricing discussions
- Personal opinion content (save for personal TikTok)
- Direct booking CTAs — LinkedIn is relationship-first, transaction-second

**Target venues:**

## Proactive Outreach Calendar

Corporate buyers plan 4–12 months in advance. TC needs to be in their pipeline before they start searching.

**Q1 (January–March):** Reach out to companies about summer events. "Summer team days book early in our region — wanted to reach out before the calendar fills." Technology companies, real estate firms, and healthcare employers respond well in January/February.

**Q2 (April–May):** Follow up with any Q1 leads. Begin reaching out for fall corporate events and client appreciation events (October/November are peak). Also: end-of-school-year events for companies with educational or family connections.

**Q3 (July–August):** Reach out for holiday party planning. "Our December calendar starts filling in August for companies that want something unique." This is also when annual event partners should hear from TC about next year.

**Q4 (October–November):** Follow up with anyone who didn't book this year. "We have two spring dates still available — wanted to reach out before we open the calendar publicly." This is the start of the next year's corporate sales cycle.

**Relationship maintenance:** Any company that has booked TC once should hear from Stacie personally 6 months after their event — not a marketing email, a personal note. That's how annual partnerships develop.

## Pricing Language for Corporate

### Anchoring Principle
Never present pricing as if it is negotiable. Corporate buyers expect tiered pricing, not a negotiation. Present it as a menu.

### How to Talk About Price
- "Our corporate team events start at [rate] for groups up to 25. Larger groups and custom retreat formats are quoted by proposal."
- Never: "We're flexible on price" or "We can work within your budget"
- Never: apologize for the price or pre-emptively justify it

### ROI Framing
For corporate buyers, frame the cost against alternatives:
- "A catered team lunch for 30 runs $800–$1,200 with no experience. TC is $[rate] with professional photos they keep."
- "Compared to a ropes course or axe throwing venue, TC provides a custom-branded photo deliverable — something they actually take home."

## Long-Term Partnership Model

The highest-value corporate relationship is one that recurs annually without a re-pitch.

### Annual Event Partnership Structure
After a company's first event with TC:
1. Send a post-event recap within 1 week (photos, summary, instant photo delivery stats)
2. 6 months later, send a brief "early bird" message for next year's availability
3. Offer priority scheduling and, for 2+ year partners, a rate lock or a small value add

### What to Track
- Company name, HR/event contact name and email
- Date(s) of event(s), group size, service provided
- Any feedback received
- Status: one-time, returning, or annual partner

### Corporate Case Study Format

After TC's third corporate event, build one case study. Use this format:

**One-Page Corporate Case Study (for media kit and website)**
- **Company:** [Type of company — no name without permission] | [Approximate size] | [Industry]
- **The brief:** What they were planning and what they asked for
- **What TC delivered:** Services, duration, group size, instant photo delivery stats (number of photos delivered, scan rate)
- **One quote from the client:** HR manager, event coordinator, or executive — one sentence, genuine
- **What they left with:** Custom-branded photos, the experience itself, the story they now tell
- **Result:** Any notable follow-up (re-booking, referral, social media share from the company)

A case study like this is worth more than ten testimonials. One real, specific example of TC executing a flawless corporate event builds more trust than any amount of general praise. Build it intentionally.
