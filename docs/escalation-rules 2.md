# Tribal Cowboy — Escalation Rules

**Last updated:** 2026-03-21
**Business:** Tribal Cowboy LLC — Stacie Huffhines, Nisenan Maidu
**Purpose:** Define what requires manual review, how to hold the space while review happens, and how to resolve sensitive situations without damaging TC's reputation or legal position.

---

## Core Principle

When in doubt, escalate. A delayed response handled correctly is better than an immediate response handled wrong. These rules exist to protect TC financially, legally, and reputationally — and to protect the people TC works with.

---

## Full List of Manual Review Triggers

Any of the following requires a pause before full response. Send the holding message first, then review.

### Financial and Booking
- [ ] Refund request — any dollar amount
- [ ] Pricing dispute (client believes they were quoted differently)
- [ ] Deposit dispute or chargeback
- [ ] Request to waive or reduce fee after booking is confirmed
- [ ] Request for cancellation with a claim to full or partial refund
- [ ] Booking confirmation not received by client (client says they never heard back)

### Safety and Animal Welfare
- [ ] Client reports injury at a TC event
- [ ] Client reports their child was scared, hurt, or distressed in a way that goes beyond normal
- [ ] Animal welfare complaint — any claim about how TC's animals are treated
- [ ] Any message that references calling animal control or animal welfare organizations
- [ ] Safety concern about a specific event or handling procedure

### Legal and Liability
- [ ] Any message that uses the words "lawyer," "attorney," "sue," "legal action," "report," or "file a complaint"
- [ ] A request for a signed agreement, waiver, or contract modification mid-booking
- [ ] Media or publication requesting rights to use TC's photos or video without payment
- [ ] Anyone claiming copyright over TC's content
- [ ] Complaints that could result in a regulatory inquiry (event permits, zoning, animal transport)

### Reputation and Controversy
- [ ] A negative review being posted publicly on Google, Yelp, or Facebook
- [ ] A public social media complaint tagging @TribalCowboy or referencing the business by name
- [ ] A claim that TC's Indigenous identity is being misrepresented or exploited
- [ ] Any message from a competitor making claims about TC
- [ ] Anything that involves a third-party vendor making TC look bad (venue, catering, photography partner)
- [ ] A thread or comment that is gaining momentum in a negative direction

### Media and Press
- [ ] Any journalist, publication, podcast, or documentary inquiry
- [ ] Requests to be featured in a piece about Indigenous business, horses, or North Idaho
- [ ] Unsolicited press coverage (someone wrote about TC without reaching out first)
- [ ] Any request for a statement or response for publication

### Partnership and Contracts
- [ ] Any brand offering a paid partnership that requires a formal agreement
- [ ] Any contract or terms of service requiring Stacie's signature or legal commitment
- [ ] An affiliate agreement that requires exclusivity or category restrictions
- [ ] A partnership offer that appears to misrepresent TC's audience, identity, or capabilities

### Emotional and Interpersonal
- [ ] A message that is emotionally escalated — the person is clearly upset or distressed
- [ ] A former client expressing ongoing grievance after a previous resolution was attempted
- [ ] Messages with threatening language of any kind
- [ ] Situations where a client or follower appears to be in personal crisis (mental health signals, emergency signals — respond with care and refer to emergency services if needed)

## Escalation Response Templates

These are holding responses — they buy time for a thoughtful reply. They are warm, professional, and do not admit anything or commit to anything.

### Template 1 — General Escalation Hold
Use when: something requires review but you cannot respond immediately

"Thanks for reaching out. I want to make sure I give you a thorough and accurate response — I'll get back to you within [timeframe]. If you need to reach me directly in the meantime, info@tribalcowboy.com is the best way.

— Stacie"

### Template 2 — Refund or Booking Dispute Hold
Use when: someone is requesting a refund, cancellation with refund, or disputing a charge

"I hear you and I want to resolve this properly. Let me pull your booking details so I'm looking at the right information — can you confirm your name and event date? I'll follow up directly within [timeframe].

— Stacie"

**What this does:** It acknowledges, it gathers information without admitting anything, and it commits to a follow-up time.

### Template 3 — Safety or Injury Hold
Use when: a client reports an injury or distressing experience

"I'm really sorry to hear this — this is not something I take lightly. I'm going to look into this right away. Can you send me your contact information and the details of what happened? I want to speak with you directly.

— Stacie"

**Note:** Do not use the word "liability" in this message or any message related to injury. Do not admit fault. Do not offer compensation before understanding what happened. Get the facts first.

### Template 4 — Legal Language Hold
Use when: someone mentions lawyers, legal action, or formal complaint

"I understand you're frustrated, and I want to address this properly. I'm going to take some time to review the details of your situation and respond thoughtfully. I'll be in touch within [timeframe].

— Stacie"

**What this does:** It does not panic, does not escalate, does not admit or deny. It buys time to understand the situation.

### Template 5 — Media or Press Hold
Use when: a journalist, publication, or podcast reaches out

"Thank you for reaching out — this sounds like something I'd like to learn more about. Can you send me more details: the piece you're working on, your publication and timeline, and what you'd need from TC specifically? I'll follow up from info@tribalcowboy.com.

— Stacie"

**What this does:** It expresses genuine interest without committing to participation. Review the opportunity before agreeing.

### Template 6 — Contract or Partnership Hold
Use when: a brand or partner sends a formal agreement or contract language

"Thanks for sending this over — I want to review it carefully before responding. I'll get back to you within [timeframe] with any questions and next steps.

— Stacie"

### Template 7 — Angry or Emotional Customer Hold
Use when: someone is clearly upset and the message is emotionally escalated

"I can hear that this is really frustrating, and I appreciate you telling me directly rather than just walking away. I want to understand exactly what happened and make this right where I can. Can you tell me a bit more about [specific detail]? I'm going to respond personally — this won't go to an automated system.

— Stacie"

## Tone Rules for Sensitive Situations

### In complaints and disputes:
- Calm and curious, never defensive
- Never match the tone of an angry message
- Do not use the word "policy" — it sounds like a corporation hiding behind rules
- Do not use the phrase "I understand your frustration" — it is overused and sounds scripted
- Do use: "I hear you," "That's not how this should have gone," "Let me look into this"

### In legal or liability situations:
- Do not admit fault before understanding the facts
- Do not offer compensation before understanding what is being asked
- Do not make statements about what TC's insurance does or does not cover until verified
- Stay factual. "What happened?" is always a safe question.

### In media situations:
- Never say anything off the record that you would not say on the record
- Do not commit to a feature, quote, or interview until you've reviewed the publication and the scope
- "I want to make sure I have the right information before I say anything" is always appropriate

### In emotional situations:
- Slow down. One message at a time.
- Do not try to solve everything in the first response — just make contact
- "I'm listening" is a complete sentence sometimes
- Know when to stop responding and provide an emergency resource if the situation warrants it

## De-escalation Language Guide

When a conversation is heating up, use language that cools it.

**Instead of:** "That's not our policy"
**Say:** "Here's how we typically handle this — let me see what I can do for you specifically."

**Instead of:** "You should have read the terms"
**Say:** "I want to make sure we're on the same page about what happened."

**Instead of:** "That's not accurate"
**Say:** "Can you walk me through what you experienced? I want to make sure I'm looking at the right information."

**Instead of:** "I can't help with that"
**Say:** "Let me see what options we have here."

**Instead of:** "You need to contact [someone else]"
**Say:** "I'm going to make sure the right person sees this — and I'll follow up with you directly."

**Never:** Use all caps, exclamation points, or anything that reads as emotional in a tense conversation.

## Documentation Protocol

Every escalation should be logged. This protects TC in future disputes and helps identify patterns.

### What to Log
For every escalated situation, note the following (even as a simple note in a doc or notes app):

- Date
- Platform / channel (Instagram DM, Facebook, email, phone)
- Person's name (if known)
- Nature of the issue (one sentence)
- Holding response sent (yes/no, when)
- Full response sent (yes/no, when, summary)
- Resolution (what happened, what was agreed, any payments made)
- Status: Open / Resolved / Ongoing

## Follow-Up Rules After Escalation is Resolved

A resolved escalation is not always a closed relationship. Depending on the nature of the situation:

**After a refund or dispute resolution:**
- If the resolution was favorable to the client: send one follow-up within 1 week to confirm they received what was promised and to close the loop genuinely
- Do not ask for a review immediately after a dispute — wait at least 30 days if you ask at all

**After an emotional or complaint situation:**
- One check-in message 1–2 weeks later: "Just wanted to make sure everything was sorted on our end — hope things are better." Nothing more required.

**After a media feature:**
- Follow up with the journalist or podcast host after publication: thank them, share the piece, offer to be a future source
- This builds the press relationship for next time

**After a partnership discussion that did not close:**
- One soft follow-up in 30–60 days if the opportunity was genuinely promising
- Do not chase more than once

**After a legal or liability situation:**
- Only communicate in writing going forward until fully resolved
- Keep documentation
- Follow up with the resolution summary in writing so there is a record
