## Creator Income Categories Available to TC

### 1. UGC (User-Generated Content) Packages
TC creates content *for* a brand's use in their own ads, website, and social channels. TC does not need a large following for this — brands pay for quality and authenticity, not follower count. This is the fastest path to creator income starting now.

### 2. Sponsored Content / Paid Posts
Brands pay TC to feature their product or service in TC's own posts. Requires audience and engagement. More accessible as following grows.

### 3. Brand Deals (Long-Term Partnerships)
Ongoing relationships where TC represents a brand across multiple posts over a season or year. Higher value, requires media kit and track record.

### 4. Affiliate Relationships
TC links to partner products and earns commission on sales. Low upfront value but compounds over time with SEO and evergreen content.

### 5. Creator Funds
TikTok Creator Fund, Instagram Creator Bonuses (when available). Requires minimum follower and view thresholds. Not the primary income target — treat as a passive supplement.

### 6. Content Licensing
Brands or media organizations pay to use TC's existing photos or videos. Particularly relevant for: tourism boards, regional publications, western lifestyle brands.

### 7. Educational / Digital Products (Future)
"How to book a corporate equine event" guides, photography templates, planning resources. Not a 2026 priority but worth noting.

### 8. Newsletter / Substack (Active)
Stacie has an active Substack newsletter. This is an underused creator income lever.

**Newsletter monetization paths:**
- Paid subscriptions (founding member tier, annual tier)
- Sponsored newsletter placements (a brand "sponsors this issue" — premium positioning, not cheap ads)
- Affiliate links embedded in relevant content
- Driving paid event bookings from email list (highest-intent audience)
- Using Substack to deepen creator credibility with brand partners (email list = owned audience, more valuable than social follower count)

**What to do now:** Mention the newsletter in the TC media kit as an owned-audience channel. Brands pay more for access to email subscribers than Instagram followers at equivalent numbers.

## What Content Themes Improve Sponsor Appeal

Brands evaluate creators on: relevance, authenticity, engagement, and whether the content style fits their campaign.

### High-Sponsor-Appeal Themes
- Behind-the-scenes of gear and equipment in real use (not staged)
- Seasonal content that shows year-round operation (brands want consistent partners)
- Educational content that demonstrates expertise (more credible than pure lifestyle)
- Personal identity content — Indigenous ownership angle opens specific sponsorship doors
- "What I actually use every day" format — extremely high authenticity signal for brands

### Lower-Sponsor-Appeal Themes (minimize)
- Pure promotional content (booking-focused posts) — makes the account look like an ad channel
- Stock-style imagery — signals inauthenticity to brand partners
- Inconsistent aesthetics — brands evaluate grid coherence

Before sending any outreach, TC needs 3–5 pieces of content that show brands exactly what they would get.

1. **Horse care context shot** — TC's herd with a gear or product integration (saddle, boot, feed, supplement). Show the real-world context, not a product hold. No "holding the product and smiling."
2. **Event-in-motion video** — 15–30 second clip of the operation running: wagon in motion, kids with ponies, the photo delivery system in action. Caption-ready, brand-safe, professionally edited.
3. **Lifestyle/identity shot** — Stacie with the horses in a way that shows her as the operator — not a prop. This is the Indigenous creator identity angle. Used for tourism, outdoor, lifestyle brands.
4. **Behind-the-work shot** — Grooming, loading, prep. Gear visible, conditions real. High appeal for equine care and western lifestyle brands.
5. **Brand-overlay demo** — A photo with the TC custom branded overlay visible, showing what instant photo delivery delivers. Perfect for event tech and photography brands.

### How to Use It
Send spec portfolio content as attachments or hosted links in outreach emails — never in the initial Instagram DM. The spec portfolio goes in a Google Drive folder organized by brand category. Update it seasonally.

## Pricing Guidance Framework

Do not publicly post a rate card. Do not quote prices in DMs before understanding scope. This is how to think about pricing.

### UGC Packages (TC creates, brand uses on their channels)
- Per photo: $200–$400 depending on usage and exclusivity
- 5-photo package: $800–$1,500
- Short video (15–30 seconds, unbranded): $600–$1,500
- UGC bundle (3 photos + 1 video): $1,200–$2,500

**Why these floors:** TC's content is not stock photography from a generic ranch. It is a working Indigenous-owned Clydesdale operation in North Idaho with a distinctive visual identity. That rarity commands a premium above commodity UGC rates. Do not undercut this. Brands that balk at these rates are not TC's brand partners.

### Sponsored Content (TC posts on TC's channels)
- Instagram single post: $300–$700 (based on engagement quality, not follower count alone)
- Instagram Reel: $500–$1,200
- TikTok post: $300–$800
- Story set (3 frames): $200–$400

**Note:** At sub-10k following, TC positions these as "brand content investment" rather than standard influencer rates. The value is not scale — it is authenticity, visual distinctiveness, niche authority, and Indigenous creator status. These are scarcity-priced assets, not follower-count-priced commodities.

### Content Licensing
- Regional single-use: $150–$400 per image
- National campaign: $500–$2,000+ per image
- Always ask what the usage is before quoting

### Partnership / Ambassador
- Seasonal (3–6 months): negotiate flat fee + product
- Annual: negotiate flat fee + product + exclusivity premium if applicable

### Rate-Setting Rule
Never quote less than 2 hours of your time at your real hourly rate. If a photo package takes 4 hours total (shoot + edit + deliver), the floor is 4 hours x $75 minimum = $300. Authenticity premium on top.

## Deal Rejection Filter — When to Say No

Not every deal is worth taking. A bad deal is worse than no deal — it cheapens TC's positioning and sets a floor that future brands use to lowball.

**Reject immediately if:**
- The brand asks TC to remove branding or post without disclosure (always decline)
- The compensation is product-only with no cash component above $300 value
- The brand's aesthetic or values conflict with TC's premium positioning (e.g., a brand that uses carnival imagery, party supply store aesthetic, or cheap western costume branding)
- The brand has a history of misusing Indigenous imagery or culture
- The contract requires exclusivity in TC's core service category (equine experiences) for less than $3,000/month
- The brand wants to repurpose content for paid ads without a separate licensing fee
- The turnaround is under 2 weeks for a multi-piece deliverable

**Consider carefully (counter before declining):**
- Low offer but strong brand fit → counter at TC's floor
- Product value strong but no cash → counter with 50% cash minimum
- High deliverable count for a single rate → negotiate per-piece pricing or cut deliverable count

**The Positioning Rule:** If accepting a deal requires TC to post content that looks cheap, rushed, or off-brand, the answer is no — regardless of the offer. One bad deal that circulates as sponsored content sets a ceiling on what premium brands will offer.
