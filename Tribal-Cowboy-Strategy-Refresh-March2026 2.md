## 1. LOCAL / NICHE LANDSCAPE

### What Families in North Idaho Actually Spend On

North Idaho families — particularly in the CDA, Post Falls, Hayden, Rathdrum, and Athol corridor — have a surprisingly limited menu of premium family experiences. The major players are:

**Silverwood Theme Park** charges $45/person general admission (ages 8-64) and $27 for kids 3-7. A family of four spends $144 just on entry before food, parking, and extras. Families in this market are accustomed to paying $150-300+ for a single outing. That is your pricing floor context — not what pony party vendors in Boise charge.

**Lake activities** (cruises, float trips, zip lines at Timberline Adventures) run $40-80+ per person. A family spending $200-400 on a lake day is normal here.

**Indoor entertainment** (Triple Play, bowling, trampoline parks) runs $15-40 per person, landing most family outings at $80-150.

The key insight: Families here regularly spend $150-400 on a single experience. You are not competing with cheap entertainment — you are competing with Silverwood, lake cruises, and zip lines. Price accordingly.

### 3-5 Competitors and Alternatives Families Consider

Your competitor research already confirmed the main finding: **there is no direct competitor in North Idaho offering horse experiences as an event service.** But here is who families consider *instead of you* when allocating their entertainment budget:

1. **Silverwood / Boulder Beach** — The default "big day out." Families budget for it seasonally. You cannot compete on scale, but you can compete on intimacy, uniqueness, and the fact that kids actually touch an animal instead of riding a machine.

2. **Generic pony party vendors (Boise market)** — Pony Party Idaho and Giddyup Farms serve the Treasure Valley, not North Idaho. They charge $200-350 for a basic 1-hour pony party with rented or small animals, no photography, no Clydesdales. They are your pricing *floor*, not your ceiling. You offer dramatically more.

3. **Mobile petting zoos** — In the Pacific Northwest, mobile petting zoos charge $300-400 for a 2-hour event plus travel fees ($1.50-2/mile). These are goats and rabbits — not Clydesdales. The experience is not comparable, but parents lump them in the same mental category. Your job is to differentiate aggressively.

4. **Bounce house / party rental companies** — The low end of the birthday party market. $150-300 for an inflatable setup. These are your "what she's already done and is tired of" competitors. You are the upgrade.

5. **Trail ride outfitters** — PNW Guided Trail Rides near Spokane does guided trail rides but has minimal engagement (4 likes per post) and does not do events, parties, or photography. Not a threat, but worth watching if they expand.

### Gaps and Unmet Needs Tribal Cowboy Can Own

**Gap 1: There is no premium, mobile horse experience between Boise and the Canadian border.** You are literally the only option. This is a geographic monopoly. Price like one.

**Gap 2: Educational animal programs for schools and preschools are wildly underserved in North Idaho.** School assembly presenters charge $625-1,250 per visit nationally. A horse-based, Indigenous-culture educational visit is worth *more* than a standard assembly, not less. You are currently undercharging for this.

**Gap 3: Corporate team-building in North Idaho has almost no unique options.** The standard is bowling, wine tasting, or a resort. Equine-assisted team experiences in other markets charge $85-200/person. You are offering something corporate planners literally cannot find elsewhere in this region.

**Gap 4: Festival and farmers market activations are priced as "vendor booth" fees, but you deliver an *experience* that drives foot traffic.** Event organizers should be paying you a premium for the draw, not charging you for the booth.

**Gap 5: The wedding and proposal market is untouched.** A Clydesdale-and-wagon ride proposal or wedding photo experience is a premium product that does not currently exist in North Idaho.

## 2. OFFERS AND PACKAGES

**What's included:** 2-hour on-site experience with pony rides (3 ponies on rotation, 45 min on / 20-30 min rest), Clydesdale photo station (families pose with a Clydesdale — fully accessible, no riding required), instant photo delivery with custom branded overlay, and a handler who adjusts the experience to every guest
**Ideal group size:** 10-25 guests
**How it should feel:** Like the birthday party every other parent talks about for the next six months. The kid who got "the horse party" wins the school year.

**Pricing: $650 base (up to 15 guests) / $750 (16-25 guests)**

*Why this price:* A basic pony party with rented animals and no photography runs $250-400 nationally. You are bringing owned Clydesdales, branded photography with instant delivery, and a level of professionalism no competitor in the region offers. $650 for a 2-hour premium experience is less than Silverwood costs for 4 people — and this is a private, personalized event. The Clydesdale alone is a differentiator worth $200+ over any pony-only vendor.

## 3. PRICING AND PROFIT

### The Undercharging Problem — Diagnosed

Based on everything in your files, here is what is happening: You are pricing based on what you think families *in your area* can afford, filtered through a fear of being "too expensive." But the data tells a different story.

Families in North Idaho spend $45/person at Silverwood without thinking twice. They spend $200+ on a lake day. They pay $300-400 for a mobile petting zoo with goats and rabbits. You have Clydesdales — two mother-daughter pairs that no other business in a 100-mile radius can offer — instant photo delivery, Indigenous cultural depth, and a level of professionalism documented in your EAP and partnership agreement that rivals operations three times your size.

You are not a petting zoo. You are not a bounce house. You are a premium, one-of-a-kind equine experience with a technology differentiator (instant photo delivery), a cultural differentiator (Nisenan Maidu ownership), and a geographic monopoly.

### Pricing Logic: Three Rules

**Rule 1: Anchor to the Experience, Not the Industry**

Do not look at what pony party vendors in Boise charge and price yourself there. Look at what families spend on a premium experience in your market ($150-400) and price yourself as the best version of that. Your anchor comparison is "a memorable family experience" — not "pony rides per hour."

**Rule 2: Build in Profit Protection with Base Fees**

Never price per head for family events. Per-head pricing lets the customer control your revenue. A base fee of $650 guarantees your floor regardless of whether 10 or 15 kids show up. Per-head pricing only works for large events (50+ people) where it creates an upsell opportunity.

**Rule 3: Use Add-Ons to Capture Willingness to Pay**

Your base packages should be profitable on their own. Add-ons (extra hour, wagon rides, custom signage, additional animals) let the customers who want more pay more without you having to create entirely new packages. Add-ons are nearly pure profit because your setup cost is already covered.

| Wagon & Wonder (seasonal rides) | $1,200-1,800 | $17-40/rider | Wagon rides nationally: $55-70/person |

### Upsell Architecture

Every package should have at least two obvious upsells. These feel natural to the customer and protect your revenue:

**For The Full Ride:** Extra 30 min ($150), wagon ride add-on ($400), second Clydesdale at photo station ($200)
**For Little Wranglers:** Take-home mini horse plush ($8/student, you buy wholesale for $3-4), parent/teacher photo session after ($150)
**For Boots on the Ground:** Branded event signage ($200), executive team Clydesdale portrait session ($300), video highlight reel ($500)
**For Show Up & Shine:** Sponsor-branded photo overlays ($300), wagon rides ($500), extended hours ($400/hr)
**For Wagon & Wonder:** Holiday-themed decor upgrade ($200), hot cocoa/cider station partnership ($150), photo package with instant delivery ($300)

### How Not to Underbid Yourself — A Quick Decision Filter

Before quoting any price, run it through this filter:

1. **Time cost (your floor, not your price):** How many total hours will this take (load, drive, setup, event, teardown, drive home, admin)? Multiply by $75 minimum. If the quote is below that number, you are losing money — raise it. But this is your *floor*, not your price. Your price should be set by the experience value (Rules 1-2 above). The time cost filter is a safety net, not a pricing strategy.
2. **Uniqueness premium:** Is anyone else in North Idaho offering this? If no (and the answer is almost always no), add 25-40% above whatever you'd charge if there were competitors.
3. **Hassle factor:** Remote location? Difficult access? More than 45 minutes of drive time? Add a travel fee ($2/mile round trip) and don't feel bad about it.
4. **Repeat potential:** Is this a one-time event or a potential recurring client? For first-time corporate or venue clients, hold your price — the first booking sets the anchor for every future one.
