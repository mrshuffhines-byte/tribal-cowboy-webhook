# Tribal Cowboy — Engagement Routing Rules

**Last updated:** 2026-03-21
**Business:** Tribal Cowboy LLC — Stacie Huffhines, Nisenan Maidu
**Purpose:** Master decision guide for every inbound comment, DM, and Story reply across all platforms.

---

## Why This Document Exists

Every message that comes into TC is an opportunity. The wrong response loses a booking, a brand deal, or a relationship. The right response builds trust, moves people forward, and keeps TC's voice consistent whether the inbox is handled by Stacie or a future assistant.

These rules are not about being robotic. They are about being intentional.

---

## Classification System — 11 Message Intent Types

Before responding to anything, classify it. This takes 5 seconds and determines which tier and which response approach to use.

### Intent Type 1: Casual Engagement
**Definition:** The person is expressing appreciation, reaction, or connection. They are not asking for anything. They are not a lead.
**Signals:** Emoji reactions, single-word responses, "this is so cute," "love this," "your horses are beautiful," "I need one"
**TC Examples:**
- "Duke is gorgeous 😭"
- "This looks so fun"
- "I wish we had something like this near us"
- "My daughter would lose her mind"

### Intent Type 2: Booking Inquiry
**Definition:** The person wants to book TC or is gathering information to make a booking decision for a personal or family event.
**Signals:** Questions about availability, pricing, group size, how it works, pony parties, "do you come to [location]"
**TC Examples:**
- "How much is a pony party?"
- "Do you do birthday parties? My daughter is turning 5"
- "Are you available in August?"
- "What's included in the wagon ride?"
- "Can you come to Spokane?"

### Intent Type 3: Corporate or Group Inquiry
**Definition:** The person is inquiring on behalf of a business, organization, or large group — not a personal event.
**Signals:** Words like "our team," "company event," "corporate," "HR," "retreat," "how many people can you accommodate for a larger group"
**TC Examples:**
- "We're planning our company picnic and saw your page — do you do corporate events?"
- "I'm an event coordinator for a healthcare company in CDA. Is this something you could do for 50 people?"
- "Our leadership team is doing a retreat in North Idaho. Would this be appropriate?"

### Intent Type 4: Creator or Partnership Inquiry
**Definition:** The person is reaching out about a collaboration — brand partnership, content creation, feature, or creator relationship.
**Signals:** Words like "collab," "partnership," "brand," "sponsored," "ambassador," "feature," "campaign"
**TC Examples:**
- "We're a western apparel brand looking for creator partners in the PNW — would you be interested?"
- "I work with [brand] and think you'd be a great fit for our ambassador program"
- "Would you be open to a collab?"
- "I'm a photographer and would love to shoot at your location — maybe we could trade?"

### Intent Type 5: Complaint, Issue, or Sensitive Situation
**Definition:** The person is expressing dissatisfaction, frustration, concern, or making a claim that requires careful handling.
**Signals:** Words like "unhappy," "refund," "disappointed," "didn't show up," "not what I expected," "I want my money back," direct criticism
**TC Examples:**
- "We had a booking and never heard back — this is unacceptable"
- "The ponies were smaller than what the photos showed"
- "I'm waiting on a refund for my canceled event"

### Intent Type 6: Media, Press, or Professional Outreach
**Definition:** The person is a journalist, blogger, content creator, or industry professional reaching out for a professional reason other than booking or partnership.
**Signals:** "I'm a journalist," "I write for," "we're doing a story on," "would love to feature TC," "podcast," "documentary"
**TC Examples:**
- "I write for Idaho Magazine and we're doing a piece on Indigenous-owned businesses"
- "I have a podcast about equine entrepreneurs and would love to have you on"
- "We're producing a documentary about North Idaho and Tribal Cowboy came up"

### Intent Type 7: General Question (Non-Booking)
**Definition:** The person is curious or seeking information but is not yet a lead. They want to understand something about TC, horses, the region, or the operation — not to book.
**Signals:** "How do you..." / "What kind of..." / "Do the horses..." / "How does instant photo delivery work?" / "What's the difference between a pony and a mini?"
**TC Examples:**
- "How big is a Clydesdale compared to a regular horse?"
- "How does instant photo delivery actually work? Like, do you need an app?"
- "Do the ponies stay calm around big crowds?"

### Intent Type 8: Pricing Interest (Pre-Booking)
**Definition:** The person is asking about cost before they've indicated they're actually booking. They're evaluating whether TC is in their price range, not confirming a booking.
**Signals:** "How much..." / "Is it expensive?" / "What's your pricing like?" / "Do you have packages?" — without a specific date, event, or group size attached
**TC Examples:**
- "How much does a pony party cost, roughly?"
- "Are your corporate events pricey?"
- "Just curious what the range is"
**Note:** Distinguish from Booking Inquiry (Type 2), which includes a specific event, date, or clear booking intent.

### Intent Type 9: Creator or UGC Opportunity
**Definition:** A brand or platform is reaching out about paid content creation, UGC, affiliate, or monetization — distinct from a creator-to-creator collab (Type 4).
**Signals:** "We're looking for creators to..." / "Would you be interested in creating content for..." / "We have a paid partnership opportunity..." / "Our affiliate program..."
**TC Examples:**
- "Hi, we represent [brand] and are looking for UGC creators in the equine space"
- "We have a paid content opportunity for western lifestyle creators"
- "Would you be interested in joining our affiliate program?"

### Intent Type 10: Sensitive or High-Risk Message
**Definition:** Any message where the wrong response creates legal, financial, reputational, or personal risk — even if it doesn't look like a complaint.
**Signals:** Legal language / threats / requests for guarantees / unusual claims / someone who appears to be documenting a dispute / messages about TC from non-customers
**TC Examples:**
- "I was injured at one of your events and..."
- "I'm going to leave a review saying..."
- "My attorney has advised me..."
- "I've been waiting [X weeks] for a response and I want it in writing that..."

### Intent Type 11: Compliment + Follower Engagement (Distinct from General Casual)
**Definition:** High-warmth engagement from a follower who is building a relationship over time — not a first-time commenter, and not asking for anything. This person is an advocate in formation.
**Signals:** Regular commenter, consistent liker, someone who tags others repeatedly, someone who has been following for a while and shows up often
**TC Examples:**
- A commenter who always says something personal and engaged on every post
- Someone who has tagged friends multiple times across different posts
- A follower who responds to stories consistently

## Response Tiers

### Tier 1 — Warm Engagement Only

**When to use:** Intent Type 1 (Casual Engagement)
**Goal:** Build relationship, signal humanity, make the person feel seen. No selling.

**Tone rules:**
- Match their energy without being over-the-top
- Reference something specific from their comment (not just "Thank you!")
- One or two sentences is almost always enough
- Never add a CTA to Tier 1 responses — it reads as desperate

**What to include:** Acknowledgment, personality, specificity
**What never to include:** Booking links, price mentions, "DM us to book," "check out our website"

**Example scenario:** Someone comments "Duke is everything 😭" on a Clydesdale post
**Response:** "He knows it too, which is honestly the problem. 😄 Duke has the most patient soul of any animal I've ever worked with."

**Example scenario:** Someone comments "I wish my daughter could meet your ponies"
**Response:** "Someday maybe she will — the ponies are absolute chaos in the best way."

---

### Tier 2 — Warm + Soft Website Mention

**When to use:** Intent Type 1 when there is a natural opening, or when a comment implies strong interest without a direct question
**Goal:** Move interest slightly forward without being pushy

**Tone rules:**
- Still feels like a conversation, not a sales response
- Website mention is casual, not urgent
- Only if it flows naturally — if in doubt, use Tier 1

**What to include:** Warm response + one organic reference to website or bio link
**What never to include:** Pricing, urgency language, multiple CTAs

**Example scenario:** "My daughter would absolutely love this for her birthday 🥺"
**Response:** "Birthday pony parties are one of my favorite things we do — the reaction when the ponies arrive is something else. Info is at www.tribalcowboy.com if you want to take a look."

**Example scenario:** "We did something like this at a company event once and it was a huge hit"
**Response:** "Corporate events are a big part of what we do — it's one of those things where people who were skeptical end up being the ones who don't want to leave. tribalcowboy.com has the info if it's ever relevant."

---

### Tier 3 — Warm + Inquiry Direction

**When to use:** Intent Type 2 (Booking Inquiry)
**Goal:** Give them enough information to move forward, direct them clearly, keep the tone human

**Tone rules:**
- Warm but efficient — they asked a question, answer it
- Direct them to the right next step without over-explaining
- Never launch into a full sales pitch in the comments
- If pricing question: acknowledge it, give a starting point or invite them to email/DM for details

**What to include:** Brief answer, clear next step (DM, website, or email)
**What never to include:** Full pricing breakdowns in public comments, urgency pressure, multiple links

**Example scenario (Instagram comment):** "How much do you charge for a pony party?"
**Response:** "DM me and I'll send you the full info — depends on guest count and location. Happy to help figure out what works."

**Example scenario (Instagram DM):** "Hi! Are you available in July for a birthday party? My daughter is turning 6."
**Response:**
"Hi! July is a great time for a pony party — glad you reached out.

Can you tell me more? Approximate date, your location (we're based in Athol but travel within the region), and rough guest count. I'll check our calendar and send you what we'd need to make it happen.

— Stacie"

**Example scenario (Facebook comment):** "Do you come to Spokane?"
**Response:** "We do serve the Spokane area — send us a message with your event details and we'll figure out if it works."

---

### Tier 4 — Corporate or Partnership Advancement

**When to use:** Intent Type 3 (Corporate/Group) or Intent Type 4 (Creator/Partnership)
**Goal:** Move the conversation forward professionally. This is a warm lead — handle it accordingly.

**Tone rules:**
- Professional but not stiff — TC's voice does not change for corporate leads
- Be specific and responsive to what they actually asked
- For corporate: gather enough info to send a real proposal
- For partnerships: express genuine interest, not desperation; ask a clarifying question before pitching back

**What to include:** Acknowledgment of their specific situation, questions to clarify scope, clear next step
**What never to include:** Generic "thanks for reaching out" template energy, over-eager yes-to-everything, pricing before understanding scope

**Example scenario (Corporate DM):** "We're planning a company event for about 40 people and saw your page. Do you do corporate groups?"
**Response:**
"Yes, absolutely — corporate events are a significant part of what we do and 40 is a great group size for what we offer.

A few quick questions: What's your general timeframe? Are you looking for something at your event venue, or are you open to coming to our location in Athol? And is this an employee event, client entertainment, or something else?

Once I know the basics I can put together a one-page overview with what it would look like and what it would cost.

— Stacie | info@tribalcowboy.com"

**Example scenario (Brand partnership DM):** "Hi! I work with [western apparel brand] and think Tribal Cowboy would be a great fit for our creator program. Can we talk more?"
**Response:**
"Thanks for reaching out — I appreciate you thinking of TC.

I'd love to learn more about what the program looks like. What does the collaboration typically involve, and what kind of content are you looking for? Once I have a sense of the scope I can tell you whether it's a fit and what working together would look like.

— Stacie"

---

### Tier 5 — Manual Review Required

**When to use:** Intent Type 5 (Complaints/Issues) or Intent Type 6 (Media/Press), or any message that feels legally, financially, or reputationally sensitive
**Goal:** Hold the space with a warm acknowledgment while the real response is being prepared — never auto-respond in full to anything in this category

**Tone rules:**
- Calm, not defensive
- Acknowledge without admitting fault (for complaints)
- Express interest without over-committing (for press)
- Never respond with pricing, refund terms, or policy details without review

**What to include:** Brief acknowledgment, confirmation that someone will follow up
**What never to include:** Apologies that admit liability, refund commitments, interview agreements, publication permissions

**Example scenario (Complaint DM):** "We had a booking confirmation and I never heard back. We planned our whole party around this."
**Tier 5 holding response:** "I'm so sorry — this isn't how things should go and I want to make it right. Let me pull your booking details and get back to you within the hour. Can you confirm your name and event date?"
**Then:** Escalate immediately. See `docs/escalation-rules.md`.

**Example scenario (Media inquiry):** "I'm writing for Idaho Magazine about Indigenous-owned businesses in North Idaho and would love to feature Tribal Cowboy."
**Tier 5 holding response:** "Thank you — this sounds like something I'd be genuinely interested in. Can you send me more details about the piece, your timeline, and what you'd need from me? I'll follow up from my email at info@tribalcowboy.com."
**Then:** Review the request before committing.

## Decision Questions for Every Inbound Message

Run through these in order. Stop when you have the answer.

**1. Is this casual engagement?**
If yes — Tier 1. No selling. Make them feel seen, be human, move on.

**2. Is this a booking lead?**
Is the person asking about services, availability, or pricing for a personal or family event? If yes — Tier 3. Get the information you need to respond usefully. Direct to DM or email for details.

**3. Is this a premium or corporate lead?**
Is the person representing a business, nonprofit, or large group? If yes — Tier 4. Treat it like a business conversation. Gather scope information. Offer a proposal path.

**4. Is this a partnership lead?**
Is a brand, creator, or organization offering a collaboration? If yes — Tier 4. Express real interest. Ask what they actually need before agreeing to anything.

**5. Is this a creator-income opportunity?**
Is this a brand that could pay TC for content, an affiliate opportunity, or a media kit request? If yes — Tier 4, flag for creator income review, do not auto-decline or auto-accept.

**6. Is this a support or complaint?**
Is the person expressing frustration, requesting a refund, or describing a problem? If yes — Tier 5 immediately. Hold with warmth. Do not respond fully until reviewed.

**7. Is tone risk high?**
Even if the message seems routine, does anything about it feel emotionally charged, legally sensitive, or potentially public in a bad way? If yes — Tier 5. Err toward caution.

## Platform-Specific Rules

### Instagram Comments (public)
- Respond to every comment within 1 hour of a new post going live — this is the algorithm window
- Tier 1 and Tier 2 responses only in public comments — never give pricing in a public comment
- Redirect booking and detailed inquiries to DM: "DM us and we'll sort it out"
- Never delete negative comments unless they are spam, abusive, or violate Instagram's terms — hiding or deleting legitimate concerns in public is a brand risk
- Heart reactions to all comments are fine and fast — they signal engagement without requiring a response

### Instagram DMs
- Respond within 4 hours during business hours (9am–6pm Pacific)
- First response to any DM should be human, warm, and specific — not a template block
- For booking inquiries: gather date, location, group size before giving any pricing
- For corporate inquiries: flag immediately for Tier 4 handling
- Use message requests inbox daily — new followers' DMs often go there first

### Facebook Comments (public)
- Respond within 8 hours
- Same tier rules as Instagram comments — no pricing in public
- Facebook audiences expect slightly more detail than Instagram — a longer reply is acceptable
- React to all comments (Like or Love) as a minimum baseline

### Facebook DMs
- Respond within 8 hours
- Facebook users who DM tend to be more intent-forward — take them seriously
- Use the same tier routing system as Instagram DMs
- Facebook Messenger has auto-reply options — if using them, set a warm holding message that does not sound like a robot

### Story Replies
- Story replies (swipe-up DM responses to a story) are almost always high-intent or high-warmth
- Respond within 24 hours
- If the reply is "where do I book this" or similar high-intent: Tier 3 immediately
- If the reply is emotional or relational (someone sharing a personal horse story, a compliment): Tier 1 — invest in this relationship

### Comments on Ads or Boosted Posts
- Check ad comments more frequently — they are often lower-quality but occasionally catch a real lead
- Spam: hide or delete
- Genuine inquiry: Tier 3, DM redirect
- Complaints about seeing the ad: ignore or hide — do not engage defensively

## General Rules Across All Platforms

1. Never argue with anyone publicly, regardless of what they say
2. Never make promises in DMs that have not been confirmed (availability, pricing, travel range)
3. Never use the word "unfortunately" — it sounds corporate and cold
4. Never start a response with "Hi there" — say their name if you have it, or just start talking
5. If you do not know the answer, say so and say when you will have it — do not guess
6. Every response should sound like it came from a person, not a customer service system
