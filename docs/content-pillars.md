# Tribal Cowboy — Content Pillars

*Eight pillars. Every post belongs to one. Never post the same pillar back to back. Max one Pillar 5 post per five posts.*

---

## Pillar 1: The Animals

### Description
Real horses have real personalities. This pillar is about the individual animals — specific names, specific behaviors, specific observations. Not "our beautiful horses." The specific 22-year-old mare who still runs this herd. The mini who acts like a stallion in a body that doesn't back it up. The proud-cut gelding who has appointed himself manager of everything.

The TC herd, broken down by animal and content angle:

**Clydesdales:**
- **Abby** (Gor-Dey Abby, 7, bay) — bred in Canada, daughter of Jill. The younger half of the mother-daughter pair. Her personality is still being documented — every specific detail earns a post.
- **Millie** (5, Clydesdale mare) — daughter of Belle. The younger end of the other mother-daughter pair. Millie is the one who's been called "unbothered" — she ignores what doesn't interest her.
- **Jill** (Gor-Dey Twin Pines Jill, 13, bay) — Abby's dam. Same Canadian breeder as Abby. At 13, she's the experienced middle of the herd. Mother-daughter pair with Abby.
- **Belle** (Turpie's Tally Annabell, 22, bay) — Millie's dam. The senior matriarch. 22 years old. She has opinions about breakfast timing and is not shy about them. Her age is always worth noting. Mother-daughter pair with Millie.

**The mother-daughter angle:** TC has two mother-daughter Clydesdale pairs — Jill & Abby, and Belle & Millie — all in the same active herd. This is not common. No other North Idaho equine business can say this. Use this framing when introducing the herd or posting about any of the four.

**Minis — the older pair (at Stacie's house):**
- **Monster Unicorn** (~10–11, gelding) — the shedding season horse. His spring shed produces relatable, funny content for horse people. Post a Monster shedding video in March/April.
- **Master Chief** (~11–12, proud-cut gelding) — one year older than Monster, acts like he runs the whole operation. The proud-cut detail means he has stallion attitude in a mini's body. That gap is the content angle.

**Minis — 2023 trio (same breeder, age ~3):**
- **Reba, Dolly, Lainey** — three mini mares named after country legends (Reba McEntire, Dolly Parton, Lainey Wilson). Introduce them as a trio. The names do the work. There is a post series here: "Meet Reba. Meet Dolly. Meet Lainey." Three posts, three personalities, the country legend angle is the hook.
- **Minimus** — mini stallion, cryptorchid, age ~3. Stallion energy, mini body. The name is the joke and the description simultaneously. Horse people will understand immediately; everyone else will ask.

**Stacie's personal horses:**
- **Jolene** (grade mare, likely QH, 10) — named after the Dolly Parton song, which means TC's herd has an unintentional country music theme. Worth noting exactly once, with humor.
- **Rowdy** (Paint pony, 13.1hh, gelding, 5–6) — currently at training. At 13.1hh he sits right at the pony/horse line. Strong visual content when he returns.
- **Nash** (Appaloosa stallion, cryptorchid, 5) — currently at training. Training journey content when he's further along.

### Why It Matters
Animals drive emotional connection faster than any other content. This pillar builds the audience relationship, earns follows, and creates the kind of posts people save and share. It also humanizes the brand without Stacie having to narrate herself constantly. A horse with a personality is a story — and stories spread. The TC herd has enough distinct personalities to fuel this pillar indefinitely without repetition.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Single photo with a caption that tells a small story about the specific animal
- Reel: short, 10–20 seconds, one specific animal behavior
- TikTok: hook on screen, behavior plays out, dry commentary at the end
- Instagram Stories: poll ("who stole more carrots — Master Chief or Monster?"), quick behind-the-scenes
- Series format: "Meet the herd" — one animal per post across two weeks

### Content Examples
1. "Millie has one speed. That speed is 'unbothered.' Film at 11." — caption on a video of her completely ignoring setup chaos
2. "Belle is 22. She is not impressed by your schedule. She has a schedule. It is about breakfast." — senior mare personality post
3. "Monster showed up to a kids' party and immediately identified the child most likely to share a snack. He was right." — mini personality post
4. "Master Chief is not in charge. He has not been told this." — proud-cut mini attitude post
5. "Abby's dam is Jill. Jill's dam is not here. But Abby and Jill are. That's two generations of Clydesdales in the same paddock, both bred by the same man in Alberta." — lineage/heritage angle
6. "Reba, Dolly, and Lainey are three years old and named after country legends. Minimus is also three. He has a different kind of energy." — 2023 quartet introduction
7. "It is March. Monster is shedding. This is either a content opportunity or a crime scene. Possibly both." — spring shedding humor

### Specific Post Concepts — Immediately Actionable

**Post Now (Spring 2026):**
- Monster shedding video — the spring shed is happening now. Film it. Post it.
- Belle spring content — 22 years old, still running the morning schedule

**Short-Term Series:**
- "Meet the Herd" — one animal per post: Belle → Jill → Abby → Millie → Master Chief → Monster → Reba/Dolly/Lainey together → Minimus
- "The Country Legend Herd" — Reba, Dolly, Lainey, and Jolene. One post connecting the names. Lean into the humor.
- "Mother and Daughter" — Jill & Abby together. Belle & Millie together. Two posts, same framing, one week apart.

**Seasonal:**
- Summer: Clydesdales at events — Abby or Belle at a corporate event, Millie wagon content
- Fall: Belle aging gracefully through another season — milestone content
- Winter: Jill and Abby in cold weather — Canadian-bred Clydesdales in Idaho winter is the angle

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Organic followers (converts over time)
- Families with children (aspiration + warmth)
- Horse people (credibility — the lineage and breed details earn their respect)
- Sponsor prospects (visual, emotionally engaging content is pitchable)

### CTA Options
- None required — let the post breathe
- Soft: "More of the herd at the link in bio"

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: high (best format for single-animal personality posts and series)
- TikTok: very high (animal content performs on TikTok with minimal production — Monster shedding is a natural TikTok piece)
- Facebook: moderate (shares well in community groups, horse owner communities)

### Cadence Recommendation
2–3 times per week. This is the highest-volume pillar. It feeds the audience between promotional content. The depth of the TC herd means this pillar can rotate through many different animals without becoming repetitive.

## Pillar 2: The Moments

### Description
Guest reactions. Kids meeting a Clydesdale for the first time. An adult who didn't plan to be moved, getting moved. A corporate team that went from awkward to genuinely laughing in twenty minutes. This pillar captures the emotional payoff of the experience — without over-narrating it.

### Why It Matters
This is what converts browsers into buyers. Seeing someone else have the experience makes it real. It answers the question potential customers are actually asking: "Will this be worth it?" The answer is in the photo, not the caption.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Single candid photo (the moment itself does the work — caption just adds context)
- Short reel: arrival reaction → horse moment → end shot
- TikTok: first line is the specific human moment, rest of the video confirms it
- Instagram Stories: "swipe to see what happened next" sequence

### Content Examples
1. "She stopped three feet away and just looked at Millie for a solid minute. Then: 'Is she real?' She is. Very real." — pony party kid reaction
2. "The team event was going fine until someone handed Abby a carrot and the VP of Finance completely forgot he was a VP of Finance." — corporate moment
3. "First time on a horse. Kid walked up confident. Kid walked away having whispered something private to Jolene. We don't ask." — first-horse-meeting post

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Families (converts cautious buyers)
- Corporate event planners (proof the experience delivers)
- General followers (shareable emotional content)

### CTA Options
- Soft only: "Check spring dates at www.tribalcowboy.com"
- Or no CTA — let the moment speak

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: very high (candid photo + story caption is the strongest format here)
- TikTok: high (reaction content performs well)
- Facebook: high (shares well, parents share with other parents)

### Cadence Recommendation
1–2 times per week. These posts require real guest moments — post when you have them, not on a schedule that forces manufactured ones.

## Pillar 3: The Work

### Description
Grooming at 5am. Hooking up the trailer in the dark. Setting up the photography backdrop in a field. Loading Millie when she's not in the mood. This pillar shows the actual labor behind the brand — the early mornings, the logistics, the repetition that most event businesses hide.

### Why It Matters
Showing the work builds trust and justifies the price. Customers pay more when they understand what they're paying for. This pillar also attracts the type of follower who respects craft — sponsor prospects, tourism partners, and the corporate buyer who appreciates that this isn't a side hustle.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Photo + caption (process shots work in still format)
- Short reel: time-lapse or quick-cut of setup sequence
- TikTok: POV format — "what 6am looks like when you're hauling horses"
- Stories: real-time morning prep documentation

### Content Examples
1. "Grooming Abby takes about 45 minutes before an event. That's not including the part where she changes her mind about standing still." — grooming post with specific detail
2. "Loaded by 5:30. On site by 7. First guests at 9. This is what the morning looks like." — event day logistics post, no drama, just facts
3. "The backdrop doesn't set itself up and the lighting doesn't cooperate without about 20 minutes of arguing with it. Worth it." — setup shot with dry caption

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Organic followers (intimacy builds loyalty)
- Sponsor prospects (work ethic and process are brand equity)
- Corporate buyers (reliability signals)

### CTA Options
- Usually none — let the effort speak
- Occasional: "This is what every booking looks like from our side. Check dates at www.tribalcowboy.com"

### Platform Fit
- TikTok: very high (process content thrives here)
- Instagram: high (process photos are highly saveable)
- Facebook: moderate (shares with groups interested in event planning or horse care)

### Cadence Recommendation
1 time per week. Consistent but not frequent — enough to show the work without making it the brand identity.

## Pillar 4: Indigenous Identity

### Description
This pillar is Stacie's Nisenan Maidu identity, her relationship to land, and what it means to run this business as an Indigenous woman. It is not a heritage lesson. It is not a marketing angle. It is present, grounded, and handled with the dignity it deserves — posted when Stacie has something to say, not on a schedule.

### Why It Matters
This is what makes Tribal Cowboy unreplicable. Every competitor can buy a horse. No competitor can be Stacie. This pillar builds a category of one. It also serves the creator economy and sponsor prospects — Indigenous creators are underrepresented in the western/outdoor space, and that is genuinely valuable to aligned brands.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Photo + longer caption (this pillar earns longer captions)
- TikTok: first-person, talking to camera, no script — just Stacie's perspective
- Instagram carousel: land/season photos with a caption that contextualizes the relationship to place
- Stories: passing thoughts, observations, short reflections

### Content Examples
1. "This land has been home to the Coeur d'Alene people. I work it as a Nisenan Maidu woman from California who built something here. That's a complicated thing to hold, and I'm still learning how to hold it." — reflection post, no CTA
2. "The horses don't care about any of it. The land, the history, the business. They just want breakfast. That's not an insight, that's just March in North Idaho." — lighter, grounded perspective
3. "When I tell school kids that I'm Indigenous and I run a horse business, some of them look confused. I take that as a good sign." — school program adjacent, quiet pride

### Audience Segment It Serves
- General followers (builds depth and loyalty)
- Sponsor prospects (authentic Indigenous creator voice is specific and valuable)
- Tourism partners (regional cultural story)
- Press and media (this is a story worth telling)

### CTA Options
- Usually none. This pillar does not need a CTA.
- Occasionally, for school program posts: "School programs available — email info@tribalcowboy.com"

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: high (thoughtful captions land here)
- TikTok personal: very high (first-person, unscripted)
- Facebook: moderate (good for community, but keep it shorter)

### Cadence Recommendation
1–2 times per month, maximum. Post when Stacie has something to say. Do not manufacture posts for this pillar.

## Pillar 5: Events and Booking

### Description
Upcoming events, date availability, booking windows, what's included. This is the promotional pillar. It exists to drive inquiry and booking. Keep it clean, specific, and free of pressure language.

### Why It Matters
Without booking posts, the funnel stalls. But over-promoting kills the audience relationship. The rule is 1 in 5 posts maximum. When a booking post runs, it should be the clearest, most direct post on the feed — no fluff, no pressure, just what's available and how to reach out.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Single photo (the best event photo you have) + clear text
- Stories with link sticker: direct to booking or contact page
- Facebook event post or availability announcement
- Instagram carousel: show the service, show the deliverables, final slide is the CTA

### Content Examples
1. "Pony parties are booking for May and June. Three Saturday slots still open. Parties start at $XXX — check what's included at www.tribalcowboy.com/pony-parties." — clean, direct, no pressure
2. "Corporate events available starting in April. Two-hour minimum, on-site setup, branded photography included. Email info@tribalcowboy.com to check dates." — corporate-specific availability
3. "Wagon rides for the fall season: we have availability starting in September. Details and booking at www.tribalcowboy.com." — seasonal announcement

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Active buyers (families who are ready to book)
- Corporate planners (checking if dates work)
- Executive assistants (they need this information to forward to their exec)

### CTA Options
- Always include one: URL or email address, never both in the same post
- Never include urgency language

### Platform Fit
- Facebook: very high (event and booking posts perform well here)
- Instagram: high (but keep these short — one photo, minimal caption)
- TikTok: lower (booking posts don't perform as well organically, but can run as paid)

### Cadence Recommendation
Max 1 in 5 posts. In peak booking season (March–May), you can increase to 1 in 4 — but never stack two in a row.

## Pillar 6: Education

### Description
Horse facts, breed history, instant photo delivery explanation, safety information, what to wear/bring to an event, what Clydesdales were bred for, how the wagon works, what a pony party includes. This pillar positions Tribal Cowboy as knowledgeable — not just a vendor, but a source worth following.

### Why It Matters
Educational content gets saved. Saves signal high value to the algorithm. They also attract new followers who are searching for exactly this topic. A post explaining how instant photo delivery works answers the question that every first-time buyer has, and removes a barrier to booking.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Carousel: topic broken into 5–8 slides (very saveable format)
- TikTok: quick explainer, hook is the question the viewer already has ("Ever wonder how you get your photos at a horse event?")
- Caption-forward Instagram post: 150–200 words of useful information, no filler

### Content Examples
1. "Clydesdale horses were developed in Scotland in the 1800s for hauling coal and farm equipment. Millie has never hauled coal. She has hauled a wagon full of screaming children, which is probably harder." — breed history with dry humor
2. "How instant photo delivery works: facial recognition scans your photos at the event, matches your face, sends you a link. No app download, no QR code hunting, no galleries to scroll through. Your photos find you." — explainer post, direct and clear
3. "What to bring to a pony party: closed-toe shoes (non-negotiable), a snack for yourself because the ponies will eat yours, and a phone with storage. We handle everything else." — practical pre-event education

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Active buyers removing barriers (families, corporate planners)
- General followers who are curious and not yet buyers
- Organic followers who will save and share useful information

### CTA Options
- After instant photo delivery explainer: "See it in action — link in bio"
- After event preparation post: "Questions? DM us or check www.tribalcowboy.com"
- Often no CTA — let the information stand

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: very high (carousels and educational captions are highly saveable)
- TikTok: very high (explainer content drives new follows)
- Facebook: high (educational posts share well in community groups)

### Cadence Recommendation
1 time per week. Rotate topics — don't repeat a topic within the same month.

## Pillar 7: North Idaho Life

### Description
The land, the seasons, the specific geography of this place. What the light looks like at 7am in March. What the road to the farm looks like after the first snow. What the field looks like when you're setting up in August heat. This is North Idaho as a character — not a backdrop.

### Why It Matters
Place is part of the brand. Tribal Cowboy is not transportable — it is rooted in this specific geography. This pillar attracts tourism partners and regional sponsors, and it builds the visual identity that makes the feed distinctive. It also appeals to the North Idaho and Eastern Washington audience who feel seen when their region is represented this specifically.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Single landscape or environment photo — strong image, minimal caption
- Reel: season or day documentation, no narration or light music
- TikTok: "what [season] looks like in North Idaho" — relatable regional content
- Instagram Stories: real-time environmental observation, no polish needed

### Content Examples
1. "March in Athol. Still cold enough to see your breath. The horses don't mind." — minimal caption, strong early spring photo
2. "August in North Idaho means setting up at events before the heat peaks. 7am is already warm. The horses are not pleased." — seasonal event prep with regional specificity
3. "We're in Athol, which most people haven't heard of. It's north of Coeur d'Alene. The road to the farm is gravel. The horses don't mind that either." — place introduction for new followers

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Tourism and venue partners (regional identity)
- General followers (place as community)
- Sponsor prospects (visual identity, regional distinction)
- New followers being oriented to the brand

### CTA Options
- Usually none
- Occasionally: "We're based in Athol and serve North Idaho and Eastern Washington — check services at www.tribalcowboy.com"

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: very high (visual pillar, fits feed aesthetic)
- TikTok: high (regional content attracts regional followers)
- Facebook: moderate (good for community groups)

### Cadence Recommendation
1 time per week. Mix with animal content for variety — these two pillars work well together visually.

## Pillar 8: Brand Authority and Creator

### Description
Content that signals credibility, reach, and professional identity — built for sponsor prospects, tourism partners, and corporate buyers who are evaluating the brand before reaching out. Milestone posts, media coverage, notable partnerships, event counts, credibility signals, and the creator identity behind the business.

### Why It Matters
Every other pillar builds the audience. This one tells the right people that the audience is worth something. Sponsors don't reach out to accounts that look like hobby pages. This pillar is the professional storefront that converts passive admiration into a pitch conversation.

### Post Formats That Work Best
- Milestone announcement (clean, specific, no balloons-and-confetti energy)
- Press or media feature reshare (screenshot or quote + caption)
- Event count announcement ("100 events" style)
- Creator identity post: Stacie talking about what she's building and why
- Behind-the-business post that shows scale, process, or reach

### Content Examples
1. "Somewhere between the first pony party and the 50th, this became a real business. That's not a complaint. It's just a fact worth saying out loud." — milestone post, low-key tone
2. "If you're a brand looking for a creator who shoots horses in North Idaho and actually owns them — media kit available. Email info@tribalcowboy.com." — sponsor-facing announcement, once per quarter maximum
3. "We've shown up to events in Coeur d'Alene, Spokane, Sandpoint, Post Falls, and a few places that required a real map. This is the 2025 recap." — year-in-review style post

### Audience Segment It Serves
- Sponsor prospects (proof of reach and consistency)
- Corporate buyers (credibility and scale signals)
- Tourism partners (evidence of active, regional presence)
- Press and media (story hooks)

### CTA Options
- "Partnerships: info@tribalcowboy.com"
- "Media kit available on request"
- "Corporate inquiries: www.tribalcowboy.com/corporate"

### Platform Fit
- Instagram: very high (grid presence matters for brand authority)
- LinkedIn: consider adding for corporate and B2B audiences
- TikTok: moderate (milestone content can perform if hook is right)
- Facebook: low priority for this pillar

### Cadence Recommendation
2–3 times per month. Enough to maintain professional visibility without overtaking the content mix. These posts should never dominate the feed — they support it.
