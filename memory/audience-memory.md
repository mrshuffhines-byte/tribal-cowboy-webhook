# Audience Memory — Tribal Cowboy LLC

## Overview

TC's audience is not monolithic. Six distinct segments interact with TC's content and services, each with different motivations, decision-making processes, and communication needs. Every piece of content, every response, and every platform strategy should be written with at least one of these segments as the primary target — and should not accidentally alienate the others.

---

## Segment 1: Local North Idaho Family Bookers

**Who they are:**
Parents, grandparents, and family event organizers in North Idaho and Eastern Washington. Booking for birthdays, family reunions, end-of-school parties, and kids' milestone celebrations. Predominantly women making the booking decision. Ages 28–50. Mix of income levels, but willing to spend meaningfully for a memorable kids' experience.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- Seeing real photos or video of kids interacting with horses — the authentic reaction moment is the sale
- Friend or family referral ("we went to Tribal Cowboy and it was so good")
- Price feels fair relative to the experience (not cheap, not scary)
- Easy booking process — if it's too complicated, they move on
- Confirmation that it's safe for children and well-managed

**Content that works:**
- Candid kids-with-horses moments (real joy, not posed)
- Behind-the-scenes of setup and animal care (builds trust)
- "What to expect at a pony party" style content
- Testimonials or reaction moments from real guests
- Seasonal calls to action (spring birthday season, end-of-school parties)

**Content that loses them:**
- Corporate-sounding language that feels too formal
- Overly polished content that feels fake or staged
- Hard-sell urgency pressure
- Posts that emphasize TC's Indigenous identity without a clear connection to what they're booking
- Long-form content with no visual hook

**How to talk to this segment:**
Warm, specific, reassuring. Answer their practical questions before they ask. Make it easy for them to picture their kid at this event. Never talk down to them or over-explain. Trust the visual content to do most of the work.

## Segment 2: Corporate and Business Event Planners

**Who they are:**
HR coordinators, executive assistants, office managers, and event planning professionals at North Idaho and Eastern Washington companies. Planning team-building events, holiday parties, client appreciation days, and company retreats. Booking on behalf of others — their reputation is on the line if the vendor fails.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- Professional credibility signals: polished website, clear service descriptions, liability awareness
- Evidence that TC has handled events at their scale before
- The instant photo delivery differentiator — instant branded photos is a genuine value-add for corporate buyers
- Fast, professional responses to inquiry
- A clear, low-friction proposal and booking process
- Price feels within their event budget without being so cheap it raises questions

**Content that works:**
- Corporate event setup and execution footage
- The technology angle (photo delivery demo, branded overlays)
- "What a corporate Tribal Cowboy event looks like" explainers
- Client testimonials from business events specifically
- Professional, polished brand presence across all channels

**Content that loses them:**
- Casual, overly playful content that reads as "pony parties for kids" — they need to see TC as a professional vendor
- Poor website with missing pages or weak copy
- Slow response times to inquiry
- Disorganized or unclear event descriptions
- Any content that seems chaotic or unmanaged

**How to talk to this segment:**
Professional, capable, confident. Lead with what they need to know: what TC does, what it delivers, what it handles so they don't have to. Don't try to warm them up before establishing credibility. Short, direct, professional tone in all communications.

## Segment 3: Creator Economy and Brand Partners

**Who they are:**
Marketing managers, brand partnership coordinators, PR agencies, and social media managers at brands in the equine, outdoor, western lifestyle, photography, event, family, and Indigenous business spaces. Looking for authentic creators, UGC producers, or brand ambassadors.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- Engagement quality — comments and saves matter more to these buyers than raw follower count
- Aesthetic alignment — TC's visuals and voice must fit their brand's visual world
- Audience demographics that match their target customer
- Evidence of prior content performance (viral moments, high-save posts)
- A professional media kit with clear rates and deliverables
- Authentic, trustworthy presence — brand partners don't want to associate with accounts that feel fake

**Content that works:**
- High-quality visual content that demonstrates production value
- Content categories that map to sponsorable themes (equine care, western lifestyle, outdoor family experiences, North Idaho travel)
- Consistent posting and engagement — shows the account is active and reliable
- Stacie's personal voice on Personal TikTok — creator personality is the asset

**Content that loses them:**
- Inconsistent posting that suggests the account is unreliable
- Overly promotional content that has worn out the audience's trust
- Low engagement relative to follower count
- No media kit, no stated rates, no clear partnership process
- Content that mixes brand messages without disclosure — signals compliance risk

**How to talk to this segment:**
Businesslike but warm. They're evaluating TC as a business partner. Demonstrate professionalism in the inquiry process. Have a media kit ready. Know TC's numbers. Don't undersell.

## Segment 4: Media, Press, and Regional Storytellers

**Who they are:**
Local journalists, regional magazines (Idaho Magazine, Spokane Coeur d'Alene Living, etc.), travel bloggers, podcast hosts, documentary makers, and regional tourism content creators. Looking for compelling stories about North Idaho, Indigenous-owned businesses, unusual experiences, and character-driven local businesses.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- A genuinely compelling story angle — "Indigenous woman runs Clydesdale operation in North Idaho" is a story
- Easy access to Stacie for interview or profile
- High-quality photos available for publication
- A press/media page on the website with assets ready
- Responsiveness — media move on quickly when sources don't respond

**Content that works:**
- Authentic storytelling about TC's origin, Stacie's identity, and what makes this different
- Visual content that's press-ready quality
- Content that positions Stacie as a subject matter expert on equine experiences, Indigenous business ownership, or North Idaho rural life
- Behind-the-scenes content that gives journalists material to work with

**Content that loses them:**
- Anything that feels overly promotional or like a press release
- Lack of a clear press contact or media kit
- Content that contradicts or makes it hard to understand TC's actual story
- Slow or no response to media inquiries

**How to talk to this segment:**
Open, direct, specific. Give them the story. Answer their questions fully. Don't pitch them — let TC's reality be the pitch. Respond within 24 hours to any media inquiry.

## Segment 5: The Horse and Western Lifestyle Community

**Who they are:**
Horse owners, equestrians, ranchers, western lifestyle enthusiasts, and the broad "horse girl" demographic that crosses age groups. They follow TC for the animals, the authenticity, and the connection to a lifestyle they love or aspire to. They may never book an event, but they are TC's loudest advocates and most engaged followers.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- Authentic horse content — they know the real thing from the faked thing
- Duke and the pony personalities — they bond with individual animals
- Evidence that TC takes excellent care of its horses
- Stacie's credibility as a horsewoman
- Content that makes them feel seen as part of the horse community

**Content that works:**
- Animal personality content — Duke being dramatic, pony antics
- Horsemanship knowledge (farrier visits, feed routines, health care)
- Behind-the-barn and early-morning content
- Content that shows the reality of horse ownership (the hard parts as well as the beautiful parts)
- Seasonal content tied to the ranch calendar

**Content that loses them:**
- Commercial content that reduces the horses to props
- Inauthentic or staged animal content
- Content that obviously prioritizes aesthetics over the animals' wellbeing
- Treating horse content as a backdrop for service promotion

**How to talk to this segment:**
As one horse person to another. No need to explain things they already know. Use the vocabulary of horsemanship naturally. This audience is TC's community, not just its customers.

## Segment 6: Indigenous Community and Allies

**Who they are:**
Indigenous community members, allies, people interested in supporting Indigenous-owned businesses, and those engaged in Indigenous creator or business communities. Following TC out of community solidarity, curiosity, or genuine interest in what it looks like to run an Indigenous-owned business in a specific place.

**Decision-making triggers:**
- Stacie's authentic voice — they can tell when Indigenous identity is being performed versus lived
- TC's commitment to operating with integrity
- Respect for Indigenous sovereignty, culture, and land
- Stacie as a visible Indigenous businesswoman doing something unusual and worth following

**Content that works:**
- Stacie's genuine voice on what it means to run this business as a Nisenan Maidu woman
- Content that connects TC's work to land, community, and heritage in natural ways
- Behind-the-scenes content that shows the real texture of the operation
- Acknowledgment of seasons, land, and place in ways that feel rooted, not performative

**Content that loses them:**
- Indigenous identity as a marketing angle or diversity badge
- Performative or surface-level references to being Indigenous
- Content that uses cultural imagery or language inauthentically
- Anything that feels like TC is trying to profit from Indigenous identity in an extractive way

**How to talk to this segment:**
Authentically. This is Stacie's community. The content doesn't need to be crafted for them — it needs to be true. When it's true, they'll respond to it.

## Red Flags — Content That Repels Premium Audiences

These patterns apply across multiple segments and consistently undermine TC's positioning:

1. **Urgency pressure** ("Only 2 spots left! Book now before it's too late!") — signals desperation, repels corporate and premium buyers
2. **Over-produced fakeness** — stock music, generic transitions, posed reactions — the horse community and authentic followers spot it immediately
3. **Cheap aesthetic** — low-quality thumbnails, inconsistent fonts, cluttered text overlays — damages credibility with corporate buyers and brand partners
4. **Excessive posting** — more content than TC can produce at quality repels the premium audience who expects craft
5. **Corporate clichés** — "passion for excellence," "committed to serving you" — signals a generic vendor, not a premium one
6. **Self-deprecation about pricing** — any hint that TC apologizes for its rates signals that TC doesn't believe in its own value
7. **Overly promotional feed** — more than 1 in 5 posts being direct service promotion trains the algorithm and the audience to skip TC
8. **Inconsistent voice** — sounds like one person in one post and a corporate PR team in the next — destroys trust across all segments
