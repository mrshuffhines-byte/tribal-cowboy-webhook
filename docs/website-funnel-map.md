# Tribal Cowboy — Website Funnel Map

*How social traffic should hit the website, what pages need to exist, and what each page needs to do.*

---

## Current Assumed Page Structure

Based on a standard small business website at www.tribalcowboy.com, the assumed existing structure is:

| Page | Assumed Status | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Exists | Brand introduction, services overview, CTA |
| Services (or individual service pages) | Partially exists | Describe what's offered |
| About | Exists | Stacie's story, business background |
| Contact / Book | Exists | Inquiry form or booking link |
| Gallery / Photos | Possibly exists | Visual proof of the experience |

### Problems with a Basic 4-Page Structure
- Corporate buyers can't find anything tailored to them
- Partner and sponsor prospects have nowhere to land
- Each service competes for attention on one generic page instead of converting via its own dedicated page
- There is no SEO structure — one services page cannot rank for five different searches
- The inquiry flow has no specificity — every inquiry goes to the same form regardless of what someone wants

| Wagon ride content | `/wagon-rides` — dedicated wagon ride page |

| Brand authority / creator content | `/partnerships` or `/media` |
| About / Indigenous identity content | `/about` |
| General "check availability" CTA | `/book` or `/contact` |

**Rule:** Never point every post to the homepage. The homepage is for strangers. Service-specific posts should land on service-specific pages where the visitor immediately sees what they came for.

### 1. Corporate Events Page — `/corporate-events`

**Why it must exist:** Corporate buyers and executive assistants go directly to Google, not Instagram. If there is no dedicated corporate page, they don't find Tribal Cowboy. A generic services page will not convert a corporate planner.

**What it must include:**
- A clear, confident headline: "Team Events and Corporate Experiences in North Idaho and Eastern Washington"
- What's included in a corporate booking (on-site horses, branded photography, custom overlays, instant photo delivery)
- Starting price or price range — "Corporate events start at $XXX for two hours"
- How it works: arrival → setup → event → photo delivery (3–4 steps, no jargon)
- Who it's for: team outings, company parties, client appreciation events, offsites
- One or two testimonials or event descriptions that describe a corporate booking
- One inquiry CTA: a contact form pre-filled with "Corporate Event" or an email link to info@tribalcowboy.com

---

### 2. Creator and Media Partnership Page — `/partnerships`

**Why it must exist:** Sponsor prospects, tourism boards, and media contacts have nowhere to land. Without a dedicated page, there is no place to send a partnership pitch or media inquiry — which means opportunities stall at the first touchpoint.

**What it must include:**
- A brief creator identity statement: who Stacie is, what she covers, what the audience looks like
- Platform presence summary (Instagram follower count, TikTok presence, engagement context)
- Partnership types available: sponsored posts, UGC licensing, brand ambassador, co-marketing
- "Media kit available on request" with a clear email link
- A specific note about what Tribal Cowboy is looking for in a partnership — this signals that TC chooses its partners, which is the correct power dynamic
- One clean inquiry form or direct email: info@tribalcowboy.com with subject line format suggested

### 3. Premium Experience / About the Experience Page — `/experience`

**Why it must exist:** Instagram-to-website traffic often lands on the homepage and bounces because nothing on a standard homepage is specific enough. A dedicated experience page bridges the visual world of social with the concrete world of what actually happens at a Tribal Cowboy event.

**What it must include:**
- A narrative description of what a Tribal Cowboy experience actually is — written in brand voice, not marketing speak
- instant photo delivery explanation: how it works, why it matters, what guests receive

- What a typical event looks like from arrival to departure
- What sets this apart from a generic pony party or horse event vendor
- Photos that reflect the actual candid-style work

### 4. Press and Media Page — `/press`

**Why it must exist:** As Tribal Cowboy grows toward tourism partnerships and sponsor conversations, press and media inquiries will arrive. Without a press page, these contacts don't know where to look for the story, the contact, or the assets they need.

## Premium Homepage Messaging Recommendations

The homepage has one job for a cold visitor: communicate what Tribal Cowboy is, establish that it is premium and worth attention, and point to the right next page.

**Headline recommendation:**
> Horse experiences, photography, and Clydesdale wagon rides in North Idaho — owned and operated by a Nisenan Maidu woman.

That one sentence answers: what it is, where it is, and who it's from. No clichés. No "making memories."

**Sub-headline recommendation:**
> We bring real horses to events across North Idaho and Eastern Washington. Every booking includes professional candid photography delivered through instant photo delivery instant facial recognition. No searching required.

**Homepage sections, in order:**
1. Hero image + headline + sub-headline + two buttons: "For Families" and "For Corporate Events"
2. Services overview (4–5 cards linking to individual service pages)
3. instant photo delivery explanation block (this is a genuine differentiator — explain it early)
4. About Stacie (3–4 sentences, in brand voice, with photo)
5. Social proof block (event count, testimonial quotes, notable event types)
6. Contact / inquiry CTA at the bottom

## Inquiry Flow Improvement Suggestions

1. **Single inquiry form per page.** Each service page has its own inquiry form or button, pre-labeled with the service type. No generic "contact us" forms on service pages.
2. **Required fields only.** Name, email, event type, preferred date, and message. Nothing else.
3. **Auto-response email.** Anyone who submits a form should receive an immediate acknowledgment: "We received your inquiry and will be in touch within 24 hours." This reduces the anxiety gap between submission and response.
4. **Response time commitment.** State it publicly on the contact page: "We respond to all inquiries within 24 hours, Monday through Friday." Then meet it.
5. **Mobile optimization.** Most social traffic arrives on mobile. Every form, every page, every button must work cleanly on a phone screen.
6. **Visible phone or email on every page.** Not just the contact page. Footer at minimum.

## How Social Traffic Should Be Received on the Website

Social visitors arrive warm but impatient. They followed a link from a post they liked. They want to confirm what they already suspected: that this is real, worth it, and worth contacting.

**What they need in the first five seconds:**
1. Visual confirmation that the website matches the social feed (consistent aesthetic)
2. The thing they came for to be visible immediately — not buried under navigation
3. No autoplay video, no pop-up asking for their email, no chat bubble blocking the content

**What sends them back to Instagram:**
1. A homepage that looks nothing like the Instagram feed
2. No pricing anywhere — they have to click multiple times to find a number
3. A contact form that asks for more information than a tax return
4. A website that hasn't been updated since 2023

**The goal:** When someone arrives from a TC Instagram post about pony parties, the website should feel like the next page of the same story — same voice, same quality, same specificity. If it does that, they inquire. If it doesn't, they leave and probably don't come back.
